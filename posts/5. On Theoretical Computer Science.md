After spending 2 years mindlessly following my Computer Science degree at [Western](https://www.uwo.ca/index.html), the urge for realization finally hit: why have I steered into a major that taught me nothing practical?

In this post I'd like to write about the practicality of theoretical Computer Science, especially what's in the CS curriculum for real (non-JavaScript) programmers. WIP. 

&nbsp;

### Theoretical CS Offerings
> I don't think you need a four-year degree for these topics; one semester would suffice, followed by years of actual implementations.
- **Time complexity of algorithms**. Understanding sorting efficiency.
- **Discrete Math**. Better to spend this time on Linear Algebra instead. 
- **Operating System**. Know how to use the file system and the command line. 
- **Data Structures**. Able to create, navigate and operate on a simple data structure (trees/graphs). It doesn't have to be as complicated as a red black tree (no one gives a fuck); but insertion into a binary tree should be considered trivial. I use [The Primeagen's Course](https://frontendmasters.com/courses/algorithms/) for this.
- **Databases**. Knowing concurrency is enough.
